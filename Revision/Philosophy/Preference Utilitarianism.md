Nozick's Experience Machine ^FnIxkCXr

Nozick poses a thought experiment: imagine being faced
with the chance of plugging into a machine that would
produce the experience of a very happy life, with various
pleasures, very few pains, and the experience of many
achievements. If we were to plug in, we would not know
that we are in the machine, and we would believe that our
experience is reality. However, we must agree to plug in for
life, or not at all.

Nozick argues that the majority of people would not plug into
the machine, because we value being in contact with reality,
even if that makes us less happy. He says we can't understand
this in terms of pleasure or a preference for a certain type of
pleasure, because the machine would simulate this. Instead, Nozick
argues that what we want is not a psychological state, but rather
a relation to something outside our minds.  ^zww3Sxzi

The satisfaction of many of our preferences will bring
pleasure, but many will not. Bentham and Mill do not distinguish between
producing happiness and reducing pain. But preference utilitarianism treats
these as different concepts. If people more strongly prefer not to suffer
pain than to be brought pleasure, then that would explain the thought
that it is more important not to cause harm. 

Preference Utilitarianism can argue that they offer a more
unified account of what is valuable than purely hedonistic
forms of Utilitarianism. We can continue to claim that
happiness is the only good if we understand happiness as
one's desires being satisfied.

The Preference Utilitarian can also argue that they simplify
Smart's theory. Smart argue that happiness is good, but his
understanding of happiness is in terms of those pleasures that
we approve of. Smart offers not further explanation. However,
Preference Utilitarianism can, however, say that what is good
is maximising the satisfaction of people's preferences, and so
approval is entirely irrelevant. ^CKF5DFmQ

Preference Utilitarianism ^uCUbMtbN

One solution to these complications is that we should maximise the
satisfaction of people's preferences, rather than pleasure.

If Nozick is right, then we prefer to be in touch with reality, and
having this preference satisfied is valuable. We can also appeal to
preferences to explain Mill's higher/lower pleasure distinction. We could
argue that whatever people prefer is of more value to them, whether
or not people would prefer pleasures related to thought, feeling, or
imagination.  ^U0eoI5rb
